# Introduction to the Committee
## Commission on Crime Prevention and Criminal Justice

The Commission on Crime Prevention and Criminal Justice is a policy-making body of the
United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC), which develops guidelines for
combating crime. Established on the 6th of February 1992, the commission strengthens
international cooperation and the rule of law globally. It also supports global efforts to
combat crime by advancing international cooperation to achieve safer and more just societies.

The headquarters of the CCPCJ are located in Vienna, Austria. The Commission holds annual
sessions, but intercessional meetings can take place throughout the year. The policy of the
CCPCJ is developed through resolutions and discussions. Transnational organized crime,
corruption, human trafficking, cybercrime, and money laundering are key themes the CCPCJ
deals with.

The CCPCJ consists of 40 member states, directly elected by the Economic and Social
Committee (ECOSOC). Every Commission member stays in office for three years. Seats are
divided by regions, with twelve seats for African States, nine for Asian States, eight for Latin
American and Caribbean States, four for Eastern European States, and seven for Western
European and other States. One Chair, three vice-Chairs, and one Rapporteur form the bureau
of the CCPCJ.

Introduction to the topic
Strengthening international legal frameworks to abolish the use of capital punishment.
Capital punishment, better known as the death penalty, is the legal execution of a criminal
ordered by the government. The crimes that entail this penalty are often referred to as “capital
crimes”. In many nations, these include: murder, treason, espionage, terrorism, or even drug
trafficking. This ultimate -death- penalty is deemed applicable by the states if the crimes are
thought to be severe enough.

The idea that absolute punishment is execution derives from ancient practices. It was used
throughout the world, including Europe, ancient Mesopotamia, Imperial China, and many
other regions. Even in the Code of Hammurabi, written four thousand years ago, there are
crimes listed deemed punishable by death. However, in the more recent part of history, people
argued it was incompatible with human dignity and, more recently, the basic human right to
life. These ideas played a great role in forming the modern-day human-rights laws.

The reason why this is such a debated topic internationally is because of the United Nations.
After World War II, the international community made an effort to create institutions and
treaties protecting fundamental human rights. The right to life was one of the biggest topics
back then. They created the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR),
which limited the use of capital punishment. They also created the First and Second Protocols
to the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR), whose main objective
was to promote the abolition of capital punishment and serve justice worldwide.

In today's world 3 types of governments can be observed when it comes to capital
punishment. Firstly, there are those who have banned, therefore completely abolished, the
death penalty. Secondly, some have only abolished it in practice, but it stays legalized. And
lastly, there are those who still actively carry out executions. These were all observed by
Amnesty International, and they have found that more than two-thirds of today's governments
have left the use of the death penalty behind.

Causes

The main idea behind the use of capital punishment is that it is the best way to discourage
serious crime, however, many studies contradict this claim. The other reason why it is still
used in some regions is that governments argue that it’s a traditionally acceptable way of
serving justice. They argue that criminal law should remain a domestic matter, and the
international community cannot force them to comply with these modern norms.

However, the people who are striving for the abolition of the death penalty argue that many
of the nations that still use it have underdeveloped judicial systems. Wrongful convictions,
forced confessions, and unfair trials happen every day, and the death penalty makes the
sentence served by the victim irreversible.

There have been many instances where DNA results revealed a wrongful conviction after the
death penalty had already been carried out, ultimately resulting in the abolishment of the
death penalty in most nations with developed judicial systems.

Strongly affected regions
In 2025, a significant increase in the use of capital punishment was determined, especially
because of drug offenses. Regions where people are affected by capital punishment are Iran
(1.500 reported executions), Saudi Arabia (356 reported executions), the United States of
America (47 reported executions), Somalia (24 reported executions), Singapore (17 reported
executions), and Afghanistan (4 reported public executions). Additionally, in the Democratic
People’s Republic of Korea and China, exact numbers are difficult to obtain, as the use of
capital punishment remains secret.

Latest Developments
The global abolition of capital punishment shows a mixed pattern. On the one hand, the use
of capital punishment increased drastically in the last year, especially in countries like Iran,
Saudi Arabia, and the USA. In 2025, the number of executions in Iran more nearly doubled
compared to 2024. Norwegian-based Iran Human Rights (IHR) group estimates 1500
executions till the start of December, while the number is never completely clear, as the
Iranian authorities do not give official figures. In 2024 roundabout 975 executions could be
verified, which shows the drastic increase in comparison to 2025.

On the other hand, despite these negative developments, Zambia and Ghana abolished capital
punishment in 2023, followed by Zimbabwe in 2024. Additionally, Malaysia reduced the
scope of capital punishment, while full abolition is scheduled for 2026. In total, 122 of all
countries have abolished the death penalty successfully in law, with 113 for all crimes, while
nine other countries have abolished it for ordinary crimes. Another 23 countries have not
practiced the death penalty for at least a decade. Those are called de facto abolitionists.

In conclusion, the pressure to abolish the death penalty increased, with a record 130 countries
voting in favor of a UN resolution calling for a global moratorium on executions on the 17th
December 2024. Despite this pressure, executions increased in Iran, the USA, and Iraq. The
implementation of an abolition is a slow but continuing global trend.

Measures taken by the United Nations (UN)
The United Nations (UN) has made it clear that it's one of its own to limit and ultimately
fully abolish capital punishment. There have been many institutions and treaties to back up
this claim.

International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR):
This treaty is one of the core human-rights treaties implemented by the United Nations) UN.
Its main purpose is to protect fundemental human-rights such as, but not limited to: Right to
life, freedom from torture, right to a fair trial, freedom from arbitrary arrest, and equality
before the law. It was adopted by the United Nations (UN) in 1966, and it entered into force
in 1976. Countries that have ratified the ICCPR must legally commit and periodically report
back to the United Nations (UN) Human Rights Committee. This treaty does not fully ban the
use of the death penalty, however it limits it greatly. Capital punishment must only be used
for the “most serious crimes”, it must not be used on minors, and everyone must have a fair
trial and the right to appeal.

Second optional protocol:
This is one of the optional protocols connected to the International Covenant on Civil and
Political Rights (ICCPR). Its purpose is that any nation that ratifies this protocol commits to
ending goverment ordered executions and fully abolishing them within their legal systems.
Thanks to the First Optional Protocol, the citizens of said nations have a way to hold their
governments accountable and request help from the international community after exhausting
their governments' domestic remedies. The creation of this protocol was one of the main steps
taken by the United Nations (UN) to abolish capital punishment.

United Nations General Assembly (UNGA):
While the resolutions of the United Nations General Assembly (UNGA) are non-binding,
they are great at establishing international norms and placing political pressure on the
outliers. The General Assembly (GA) has adopted many resolutions that discuss the topic of
capital punishment throughout its history, most notably in 1971, 1977, 2007, and every
second year since then. This has kept the debates about the topic alive.

Possible Solutions
To combat the use of capital punishment, there are possible ways. The UN passed the
„Second Optional Protocol to the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights“ in

December 1989. This protocol aims to abolish the death penalty globally, as the death penalty
is a serious violation of human rights. „World Day Against the Death Penalty “ is observed
every October 10th. Initiated by „the world coalition against the death penalty“ this day raises
national attention to the ongoing use of capital punishment in certain countries.

Everything mentioned is a step in the right direction to strengthen legal frameworks to
abolish the use of capital punishment.
